Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about AI content verification and OriginLytics.

What is AI content verification?

AI content verification is the process of determining whether digital content was created by humans or artificial intelligence systems. OriginLytics uses advanced machine learning algorithms to detect patterns in text, images, and video that indicate AI generation. Our platform analyzes linguistic patterns (vocabulary distribution, sentence structure), statistical anomalies (perplexity, burstiness), and metadata signatures to provide a confidence score (0-100%) indicating the likelihood of AI generation. This helps publishers, enterprises, and researchers maintain content authenticity in an era where 50%+ of online content is projected to be AI-generated by 2026.

How does OriginLytics detect AI-generated content?

OriginLytics employs a multi-layered detection approach combining three core techniques: (1) Linguistic Pattern Analysis - examining vocabulary distribution, sentence structure complexity, and contextual coherence patterns that differ between human and AI writing; (2) Statistical Modeling - measuring perplexity (predictability) and burstiness (variation in sentence length/complexity) which are distinctly lower in AI-generated text; (3) Neural Network Classifiers - deep learning models trained on millions of verified human and AI-generated samples. Additionally, we analyze metadata (creation timestamps, edit history) and compare content against known AI model signatures (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini patterns). This comprehensive approach achieves 95%+ accuracy with false positive rates below 3%.

Can OriginLytics detect content from ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini?

Yes, OriginLytics can detect content generated by ChatGPT (GPT-3.5, GPT-4, GPT-4o), Claude (Claude 2, Claude 3 Opus/Sonnet/Haiku), Google Gemini, and 10+ other major language models. Our detection algorithms are continuously updated as new models are released, and we maintain model-specific signature databases. Each AI model has unique linguistic fingerprints - for example, GPT models tend toward specific transitional phrases, while Claude exhibits distinct reasoning structures. Our platform not only identifies AI-generated content but can often attribute it to the specific model family used, achieving 95%+ accuracy for major models and 85%+ for emerging/fine-tuned models.

What types of content can be verified?

OriginLytics supports comprehensive multi-modal content verification across four primary categories: (1) Text - articles, essays, social media posts, documentation, code comments, emails (support for 50+ languages); (2) Images - photographs, digital artwork, infographics, memes with support for DALL-E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion detection; (3) Video - deepfake detection, synthetic video identification, manipulation analysis; (4) Audio - voice cloning detection, AI-generated speech identification. Our platform handles various file formats (TXT, DOCX, PDF, JPG, PNG, MP4, MP3) and supports both single-file verification and batch processing for high-volume workflows. Maximum file size: 100MB per file, with unlimited files for Enterprise plans.

How accurate is AI detection with OriginLytics?

OriginLytics achieves 95%+ accuracy in detecting AI-generated content across major language models, with false positive rates below 3% and false negative rates below 5%. What is content provenance tracking?

Content provenance tracking is the process of documenting the complete origin, creation history, and modification chain of digital content to establish authenticity and detect manipulation. OriginLytics' provenance system records: (1) Creation Metadata - timestamp, authorship attribution, creation environment (device, software, location); (2) Edit History - all modifications with timestamps, editor attribution, change diffs; (3) Digital Signatures - cryptographic hashes, watermarking, blockchain anchoring for tamper-proof verification; (4) Chain of Custody - complete audit trail of access, transfers, and processing events. This is particularly valuable for legal evidence, journalism source verification, academic plagiarism prevention, and regulatory compliance. Provenance data is stored in tamper-evident ledgers and can be exported in standardized formats (C2PA, IPTC) for interoperability with other systems.
